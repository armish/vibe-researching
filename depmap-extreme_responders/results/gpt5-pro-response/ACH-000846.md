Below is a mechanistic, evidence‑based hypothesis for why the hypopharyngeal HNSCC line FaDu is exceptionally sensitive to C7orf25 CRISPR knockout, integrating its strongest genetic dependencies and its mutational landscape.

What C7orf25 likely does
- C7orf25 encodes a highly conserved protein (UniProt Q9BPX7) that carries a C‑terminal PIN‑like nuclease domain (DUF1308) and an N‑terminal PD‑(D/E)XK‑like region. PIN‑domain proteins in mammals frequently act as RNA endonucleases in mRNA/translation surveillance pathways. C7orf25 itself has not been deeply characterized functionally, but comparative domain analyses explicitly note its PIN‑like domain and even report an interaction with threonyl‑tRNA synthetase in a high‑quality human interactome, pointing toward roles at the RNA–translation interface. ([ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gene/79020?utm_source=openai))

- By analogy, the NMD factor SMG6 is a mammalian PIN‑domain endonuclease that cleaves aberrant transcripts; this is the canonical precedent for PIN proteins executing RNA quality control in human cells. Thus, a parsimonious “working model” is that C7orf25 participates in RNA/translation surveillance or decay. ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17053788/?utm_source=openai))

Signals from FaDu’s top dependencies point to translation quality control and proteostasis
- GTPBP1 and GTPBP2 are among FaDu’s top dependencies. These translational GTPases resolve stalled ribosomes and mitigate ribosome pausing; loss of either triggers codon‑specific pausing and activates the integrated stress response (ISR). Strong dependence on both is a hallmark of cells living on the edge of translational stress. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7665888/?utm_source=openai))

- NRBF2 is also a leading dependency. NRBF2 is a stoichiometric subunit/regulator of the PI3KC3‑C1 (BECN1–ATG14–VPS34) autophagy complex and functions as a phosphorylation‑controlled “switch” for complex assembly and autophagy flux. Dependence on NRBF2 implies FaDu relies on autophagy to survive ongoing cellular stress, including proteotoxic stress downstream of faulty translation. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms4920?utm_source=openai))

- Together, the co‑dependencies on GTPBP1/2 (ribosome rescue) and NRBF2 (autophagy) are exactly what you would expect in a cell that must constantly detect/resolve defective translation events and clear the resulting protein/RNA debris.

FaDu mutations that plausibly create chronic RNA/translation stress
- Ribosome decoding: RPS15 G132R lies in the known mutational hotspot of RPS15 and is linked to reduced translational efficiency and fidelity (e.g., stop‑codon read‑through, misincorporation) in cancers. Such defects increase ribosome stalling and the need for rescue/quality control. ([academic.oup.com](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/48/3/1013/5539888?utm_source=openai))

- NMD/ RNA surveillance: UPF3A D123N affects a paralog of UPF3B; UPF3A and UPF3B act redundantly to activate NMD. Even subtle impairment of this axis, combined with FaDu’s many truncating/splice‑site variants (long list you provided), can elevate the burden of aberrant transcripts that must be policed by endonucleases/decay pathways. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9108619/?utm_source=openai))

- Autophagy‑lysosome axis: FaDu harbors a truncating FLCN mutation (S386Ter). FLCN is the RagC/D GAP that licenses mTORC1 to phosphorylate and retain TFEB/TFE3 in the cytosol; loss of FLCN drives nuclear TFEB/TFE3, boosting lysosome biogenesis and autophagy programs. Cells with FLCN loss often rely more on autophagy for proteostasis—consistent with FaDu’s strong NRBF2 dependency. ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36103527/?utm_source=openai))

- Proteasome “recovery” control: FaDu carries NFE2L1 (NRF1) L413M. NRF1 is the master transcriptional regulator of the proteasome bounce‑back response and also transcriptionally engages aggrephagy when the proteasome is overwhelmed. Even partial impairment could push FaDu to lean harder on autophagy and RNA/translation quality control to maintain proteostasis. ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29202016/?utm_source=openai))

A unifying model for C7orf25 hypersensitivity in FaDu
- Baseline state: FaDu appears to operate under persistent translation/RNA surveillance pressure due to: (i) a fidelity‑compromising RPS15 hotspot mutation; (ii) extensive splice‑site/frameshift/stop‑gain variants that generate aberrant mRNAs; and (iii) possible modulation of NMD via UPF3A. This necessitates high activity in ribosome‑rescue (GTPBP1/2) and degradative pathways (autophagy via NRBF2). Ribosome stalling is known to activate the ISR; cells in this state are exquisitely sensitive to any hit that further impairs ribosome/translation QC. ([academic.oup.com](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/48/3/1013/5539888?utm_source=openai))

- Where C7orf25 fits: As a conserved PIN‑like nuclease with suggested links to translation machinery, C7orf25 is a strong candidate component of the RNA/translation surveillance “triage” layer (akin to how SMG6’s PIN domain executes endonucleolytic cuts in NMD). In FaDu—already dependent on GTPBP1/2 and NRBF2—removing C7orf25 likely collapses an essential RNA‑QC branch, driving unresolvable ribosome stalls, ISR overactivation, and lethal proteotoxic stress. This creates an apparent synthetic‑addiction to C7orf25 in FaDu. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5499597/?utm_source=openai))

- Autophagy angle: FLCN loss pushes TFEB/TFE3 programs and increases reliance on vesicular degradation; if C7orf25 loss spikes the load of defective RNAs/proteins, the autophagy pathway (already essential via NRBF2) becomes saturated, precipitating cell death.
